Inner Ear

Anatomy / Head and Neck / Ear

Last Updated: 23rd April 2020

The internal ear consists of a series of bony cavities in the petrous part of the temporal bone, bounded laterally by the middle ear and medially by the internal acoustic meatus, and the membranous sacs and ducts within these cavities.

The internal auditory meatus is a canal within the petrous part of the temporal bone of the skull between the inner ear and the posterior cranial fossa. The internal auditory meatus provides a passage through which the vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII), the facial nerve (CN VII), and the labyrinthine artery (an internal auditory branch of the anterior inferior cerebellar artery in 85% of people) can pass from inside the skull to structures of the inner ear and face. It also contains the vestibular ganglion.

Function

The structures of the inner ear convey information to the brain about balance and hearing. The cochlear duct is the organ of hearing. The semicircular ducts and sacs (utricle and saccule) are the organs of balance.
